Naval's taxonomy of leverage has three types: labor, capital, and code. He described code and media as the final, most democratic form because they require no permission. "An army of robots is freely available," he wrote, "it's just packed in data centers for heat and space efficiency." He was right about code being a new form of leverage, but slightly wrong about what that army of robots actually produces.

What's available now isn't code as leverage. It's closer to labor leverage, but without the coordination overhead that made labor the worst form in his framework.

Naval's critique of labor was specific: managing other people is "incredibly messy," requires tremendous leadership skills, and most people who try it fail at the coordination rather than the work. I think that's fair. But AI agents don't have the same failure modes. They don't have career goals that conflict with yours, don't need performance management, and don't get offended by repetitive work. You get labor's output capacity attached to code's permissionless accessibility.

I'm not sure Naval fully accounted for this combination, and it's the most important update to his leverage framework since he wrote it.

The reason this matters for PMs is that most are currently using AI in a way that misses the leverage entirely. One chat window, one prompt, wait, read, prompt again.

Michael Gerber's E-Myth argues that most small business owners fail because they're skilled technicians who end up doing all the work themselves rather than building systems that do the work for them. That's exactly the trap here: doing the work sequentially, personally, one task at a time. It's faster than before, but it's not leverage. It's just a faster version of you.

Sub-agents change this. Instead of directing a single assistant through sequential tasks, you're running a team toward a shared outcome. The parallel execution matters because your bottleneck stops being the AI and starts being your synthesis and judgment, which is the right bottleneck to have.

Here's how to try this for a feature. Spin up three agents at the same time: one pulls together relevant customer research from notes, one drafts the spec for the next prioritized feature, one cross-references the backlog for items this decision might affect. Give each a focused brief and a clear deliverable, then review and integrate the outputs rather than executing each task yourself.

Sessions that used to take even a few hours of sequential prompting now take about 30 minutes.

I think the models are good enough. The real constraint is your mental model, whether you're treating AI as a faster version of yourself or as a team you're directing. That distinction matters more than any prompting technique, and I'm not sure most PMs have made the transition yet.
