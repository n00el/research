# 9 Claude Prompts for Stanford-Level Research

**Author:** Jainam Parmar (@aiwithjainam)
**Date:** Mar 10, 2026
**Source:** https://x.com/aiwithjainam/status/2031282913422225567

## Key Takeaways

- A structured sequence of 9 prompts can turn Claude into a literature review co-pilot that processes 40+ papers systematically rather than summarizing them individually.
- The "Intake Protocol" prompt maps the research landscape by clustering papers by shared assumptions and flagging contradictions, instead of just summarizing.
- The "Contradiction Finder" and "Assumption Killer" prompts surface hidden disagreements and untested shared assumptions that most manual reviews miss entirely.
- The "Gap Scanner" identifies unanswered research questions and suggests what methodology would be needed to close each gap -- directly surfacing publishable opportunities.
- The "Knowledge Map Builder" produces a structured outline (central claim, supporting pillars, contested zones, frontier questions, essential reads) that serves as a persistent reference artifact.
- The "So What" Test forces a jargon-free distillation of an entire body of research into one sentence of proven knowledge, one honest gap admission, and one real-world implication.
- The prompts are designed to be run sequentially, building on each other -- the Master Synthesis prompt (6) works best after running prompts 1-5.

## One-Line Summary

A 9-prompt workflow that turns Claude into a systematic literature review engine, moving beyond paper-by-paper summaries to contradiction detection, gap analysis, and structured knowledge mapping.

## Quality Assessment

**Rating: Low-medium** — Viral engagement content, not validated research.

**Strengths:**
- Sequential workflow building context across prompts is a sound approach
- Contradiction finder, gap scanner, and assumption killer target genuinely useful analytical angles
- "So What" test is a good distillation forcing function

**Weaknesses:**
- No validation — zero examples of actual output or comparison with real literature reviews
- Overpromises massively (Stanford name-drop with no affiliation, "40+ papers" unlikely to fit context)
- Ignores hallucination risk — citation chain tracing and methodology audits are exactly where LLMs fabricate
- Prompts are standard lit review steps (thematic analysis, gap analysis) repackaged as "prompts"
- Pure engagement farming format (newsletter plug, "bookmark this", "repost tweet 1")

**Verdict:** Useful as a checklist reminder of structured lit review steps. Not reliable as an actual AI workflow — hallucination risk on prompts 3, 4, 5 makes unsupervised use dangerous. Treat as a prompt template starting point, not a methodology.
