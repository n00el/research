Most AI conversations are still just text going back and forth. Claude changed that this week.

Anthropic announced that Claude can now build interactive visuals directly inside your conversation.

- Charts.
- Diagrams.
- Flowcharts.
- Widgets.

They appear right in the chat, not in a separate panel, not as a downloadable file. Just inline, as part of the response.

It is currently in beta for all plan types, free and paid. Mobile is not supported yet.

---

## What It Actually Does

When you ask Claude to explain something visual, it no longer has to rely on words alone. It can render the thing.

Ask it to visualize a compound interest curve and you get an interactive chart, not a paragraph about exponential growth. Ask it to map out a user flow and you get a diagram you can look at and react to. Ask it to compare two design approaches side by side and it builds a visual comparison on the spot.

The visuals are built with HTML and SVG. They are not generated images. That matters because they are interactive. You can click elements, adjust sliders, expand to fullscreen, and ask follow-up questions that cause Claude to update the visual in place.

The experience is closer to a whiteboard session than a document. You talk. The visual changes with you.

---

## This Is Different from Artifacts

Claude already had Artifacts, and it is worth understanding the distinction.

Artifacts are polished, permanent deliverables. You build a component, a report, a complete UI. The intent is to export it, share it, or use it somewhere else.

These new inline visuals are not that. They are thinking aids. They live in the conversation and change as the conversation evolves. They are not meant to be final outputs. They are meant to help you understand and explore faster.

Think of it as the difference between a notebook and a deliverable. Artifacts are the deliverable. Inline visuals are the notebook.

## Why This Matters for Designers Specifically

Designers already think visually. The problem with most AI tools is that they make you translate your visual thinking into text, get a text response, and then translate that back into something you can see. That loop adds friction.

This feature shortens the loop.

A few things become genuinely faster:

**Mapping a flow.** Instead of describing a user journey in words, you can say "draw this as a flowchart" and immediately have something to react to. That reaction is usually faster and more accurate than reviewing a text description.

```plaintext
Draw the onboarding flow for a SaaS app as a flowchart. The user signs up, verifies their email, completes a setup wizard, and reaches the dashboard. Show decision points where they might drop off.
```

**Comparing options.** You can ask Claude to visualize two layout approaches side by side. Not describe them. Show them. That is a fundamentally different kind of input for making a decision.

```plaintext
Show me two sidebar navigation layouts side by side. Option A has icons only. Option B has icons with labels. I want to compare how they feel at different screen widths.
```

**Explaining decisions to clients or teams.** Sometimes you need to show why a structure works, not just say it. An inline diagram inside a live conversation is a much stronger explanation tool than a screenshot or a static document.

```plaintext
Draw a simple diagram showing how a design system connects tokens, components, and page templates. I need to explain this to a client who has never worked with a design system before.
```

**Exploring data before you visualize it properly.** If you are designing a dashboard, you can drop in your data and ask Claude to chart it. Not to produce a final chart, but to see what shape the data takes before you decide how to present it in the actual product.

```plaintext
Here is my user retention data for the last 6 months. Chart it as an interactive line graph. I want to see where the biggest drop-offs happen before I decide how to display this in the dashboard.
```

## How to Use It

There is no separate mode or button to turn on. You just ask.

Some prompts that work well:

- Draw this as a diagram.
- Visualize how this changes over time.
- Show me these two options side by side.
- Map out this process as a flowchart.
- Chart this data and make it interactive.

Claude decides when a visual would help, but you can always ask directly. If the first result is not right, just say what you want changed. The visual updates in place.

The feature also connects with apps like Figma, Canva, and Slack, which opens up some interesting workflow possibilities. For now, the core value is simple: ask for a visual and get one inside the conversation.

---

## What to Expect in Beta

It is beta software. Expect inconsistencies.

Complex visuals will not always render cleanly on the first try. The more specific your request, the more accurate the output. Vague prompts produce vague results, same as any other Claude task.

The biggest current limitation is mobile. The feature is not available there yet. For design workflows, that is usually fine. Most of this work happens on a larger screen anyway.

---

## The Bigger Shift

This is not just a convenience update.

Right now, the standard design AI workflow is: think in your head, type into Claude, read the response, go build something in Figma. There is a gap between the thinking and the building.

Inline visuals start to close that gap. You can think out loud with Claude and see the thinking take shape. That is a different kind of tool than a chatbot that answers questions.

Designers who treat this as a visual thinking partner, not just an answer machine, will get more out of it.

---

## The Takeaway

Claude can now draw. Not perfectly. Not as a replacement for Figma. But well enough to change how you explore ideas before you build them.

The conversation is no longer just text. That is worth paying attention to.
