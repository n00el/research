# AI Doesn't Reduce Work -- It Intensifies It

**Author:** Aruna Ranganathan and Xingqi Maggie Ye
**Date:** February 9, 2026
**Source:** https://hbr.org/2026/02/ai-doesnt-reduce-work-it-intensifies-it

## Key Takeaways

- AI tools cause three forms of work intensification: task expansion (people absorbing others' responsibilities), blurred work/non-work boundaries (prompting during breaks and off-hours), and increased multitasking (managing parallel AI threads and open tasks).
- Workers voluntarily take on broader scope because AI makes unfamiliar tasks feel accessible, which quietly eliminates the need for additional headcount while increasing hidden workload on experts who must review AI-assisted output.
- The conversational, low-friction nature of AI prompting disguises work as casual activity, eroding recovery time and making it difficult for workers to recognize when they are overextending.
- A self-reinforcing cycle emerges: AI accelerates tasks, raising speed expectations, which increases AI reliance, which widens scope, which further intensifies work -- all while workers report feeling more productive but not less busy.
- Short-term productivity gains can mask unsustainable workload creep that leads to cognitive fatigue, impaired judgment, higher error rates, and eventual burnout.
- Organizations should proactively establish an "AI practice" -- intentional norms including structured pauses before decisions, sequenced (batched) work rhythms, and protected time for human connection and reflection.

## One-Line Summary

AI adoption triggers a self-reinforcing cycle of voluntary work intensification -- expanding scope, eroding boundaries, and increasing multitasking -- that organizations must counter with deliberate norms or risk trading short-term productivity for long-term burnout.
