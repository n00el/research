# Summary

## Key Takeaways

- **41% of corporate AI automation targets are in the "unwanted or impossible" zone** -- companies are spending billions automating the wrong tasks according to a Stanford survey of 1,500 workers and 52 AI experts.
- **Workers want AI for boring, repetitive tasks** (scheduling, filing, data entry, error checking) but companies keep pushing AI into creative work, client communication, and strategic decision-making -- areas workers want to keep.
- **The WORKBank database** maps worker desires against actual AI capabilities across 844 tasks in 104 occupations, revealing a massive mismatch between automation investment and worker needs.
- **The Human Agency Scale** shows an inverted-U pattern: workers want heavy AI for low-value tasks, shared collaboration for medium-complexity work, and full human control for high-stakes creative and interpersonal tasks.
- **Trust crisis is real:** 45% of workers doubt AI's reliability and 23% actively fear job loss -- this is a change management crisis, not just an adoption problem.
- **Skills premium is shifting:** information processing skills (data analysis) will decline in value as AI masters them; interpersonal skills (training, communication, emotional intelligence) will become the most valuable competencies.
- **Core prescription:** Stop automating what is technically possible and start building what workers actually need. Adoption depends on the humans you are trying to augment.

## One-Line Summary

Stanford's WORKBank study reveals that 41% of corporate AI automation targets are either unwanted by workers or technically impossible, exposing a massive mismatch between where companies invest in AI and where workers actually need help.
