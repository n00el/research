# The Hidden Math Behind Every Decision You Make — And Why Most People Get It Wrong

**Author:** darkzodchi (@zodchiii)
**Date:** March 7, 2026
**Source:** https://x.com/zodchiii/status/2030267008625873324

## Key Takeaways

- Expected Value (EV) is the foundational decision formula: multiply each outcome's probability by its payoff and sum them -- most people skip this and rely on gut feelings distorted by loss aversion.
- Base Rate Neglect causes people to dramatically overestimate probabilities by ignoring how rare an event is in the general population (e.g., a 99% accurate test on a 1-in-1000 disease yields only ~9% true positive rate).
- The Sunk Cost Fallacy keeps people trapped in bad jobs, investments, and relationships because they anchor on what they already spent rather than evaluating the path forward from zero.
- Bayesian Thinking means updating beliefs proportionally to new evidence -- not ignoring it, not overreacting -- and prediction markets are a real-time demonstration of this in action.
- Survivorship Bias makes success look far more common than it is because failures are invisible; always look for the denominator (how many tried vs. how many succeeded).
- The Kelly Criterion provides a mathematical formula for position sizing, but practitioners use quarter-to-half Kelly because full Kelly variance is emotionally unsustainable.
- These six models form a complete decision-making system: EV tells you whether to act, base rates ground your estimates, sunk costs tell you what to ignore, Bayes tells you how to update, survivorship bias reveals blind spots, and Kelly tells you how much to commit.

## One-Line Summary

Six probabilistic mental models (EV, base rates, sunk costs, Bayesian updating, survivorship bias, Kelly criterion) form a complete system for replacing gut-feeling decisions with structured rational thinking.
