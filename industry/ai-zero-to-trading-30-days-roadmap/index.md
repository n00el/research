## WEEK 1: Learn the Language (Days 1-7)

Most people try to learn trading by watching YouTube videos and reading Investopedia articles. That works but it's painfully slow and you forget 80% of it.

Make Claude your personal finance tutor through specific prompts rather than generic requests.

### Day 1-2: Understand what markets actually are

Use Claude, ChatGPT, or Perplexity. The author prefers Claude for extended reasoning capabilities. Use prompts with "explain like a smart friend" and "use real examples" to force practical rather than textbook responses.

### Day 3-4: Learn the math that actually matters

Skip candlestick patterns, RSI, MACD, Fibonacci retracements initially. Focus instead on four foundational concepts:
- Expected Value
- Kelly Criterion
- Base rates
- Loss mathematics

### Day 5-7: Understand the specific market you want to trade

Select ONE market: Polymarket (event betting), crypto trading, or ETFs like SPY -- whichever you understand best.

By end of week 1 you should be able to explain EV, Kelly, and base rates to a friend and understand how your chosen market works. That's it. No trades yet.

## WEEK 2: Build Your Research System (Days 8-14)

A hedge fund analyst has Bloomberg terminal, a team of 5 researchers, and an MBA. You have Claude. And honestly? For most retail-level decisions, Claude is better because it's faster, never tired, and doesn't have a boss telling it what to think.

### Day 8-9: Learn to analyze a specific market with AI

Pick a live contract and analyze it systematically using Claude prompts tailored to your market. Do this for 3-4 different contracts over 2 days. You'll start noticing patterns in how to think about probability.

That's the skill -- not memorizing facts, but building a framework for estimating odds.

### Day 10-11: Build your information sources

Create a checklist: "WOULD I BUY THIS TODAY?" Post it visibly. "I have mine on a sticky note on my monitor."

### Day 12-14: Practice analysis without trading

Analyze markets for three days without executing trades. Document:
- What you think will happen
- Your confidence level (30%? 60%? 85%?)
- Why you believe the market price is wrong
- What would change your mind

At the end of 3 days, check how accurate you were. This is called calibration and it's honestly the most important skill in trading.

By end of week 2 you have: a research system, a daily routine, a checklist, and data on your own accuracy. You haven't spent a dollar yet. That's intentional.

## WEEK 3: Start Trading Small (Days 15-21)

Now you trade. But tiny. Like embarrassingly tiny.

### Day 15: Your first real trade

Deposit $20-50 on your chosen platform:
- **Polymarket**: Bet on real-world events you already follow in news
- **Bybit**: Trade crypto (BTC or ETH only initially, not shitcoins)
- **Stock broker** (Robinhood, Interactive Brokers): Trade SPY or one stock you understand

Not $500. Not $1000. An amount where losing it all would be annoying but not painful.

Why so small? Because this week isn't about making money. It's about feeling what it's like to have skin in the game. Everything changes when real money is on the line.

### Day 16-18: Trade daily, journal everything

Pick 1-2 daily positions. After each trade, document entry price, reasoning, confidence level, and emotional state.

"After I started tracking my emotional state with each trade I realized that 86% of my worst trades happened when I was either tilted from a previous loss or bored and looking for action." That's not a math problem -- that's a psychology problem. And AI can help you catch it.

### Day 19-21: Review your first week of trading

Watch reference material on the Moneyball philosophy applied to trading: "Everyone's buying the wrong thing. Your system buys the right thing."

## WEEK 4: Build Your System (Days 22-30)

The goal of this week is to go from "I'm trading" to "I have a system."

The difference: a trader without a system makes decisions based on feelings. A trader WITH a system makes decisions based on rules. When the rules say buy -- you buy. When they say exit -- you exit. No negotiation with yourself.

### Day 22-24: Define your rules

Create personal trading rules based on weeks 1-3 learning:
- **ENTRY rules**: Specific conditions for opening positions
- **SIZING rules**: Bankroll allocation per trade (use quarter-Kelly as baseline)
- **EXIT rules**: Specific profit-taking and loss-cutting levels
- **TILT rules**: When to stop trading for the day

Make the rules simple enough that a robot could follow them. If a rule has the word "feel" in it, replace it with a number.

### Day 25-27: Run your system

Follow rules for three days without improvisation. You'll see trades that "obviously" look good but don't fit your rules. Let them go.

The discipline muscle is the hardest one to build. But it's what separates the people who make money long-term from the people who have one good week and then give it all back.

### Day 28-30: Optimize and plan forward

Review month comprehensively using AI analysis of your complete trading log.

## What Most People Get Wrong

They think day 1 should involve placing a trade. Nah. Day 1 should involve understanding what a trade even is.

They think AI will give them "picks." It won't. AI gives you a framework for thinking. The picks come from YOUR judgment trained by the framework.

They think 30 days is enough to be profitable. It might be. It might not. But 30 days IS enough to have a system, understand your own biases, and know whether trading is even something you want to pursue.

The real secret nobody tells you: the first month of trading isn't about making money. It's about building the machine that will eventually make money. The machine is your system + your psychology + your AI workflow. Once that machine works, you just feed it capital and time.

## The Prompts That Changed Everything

80% of my "trading skill" is actually prompting skill. Knowing how to ask Claude the right question is worth more than knowing any indicator or chart pattern.

Three most-used prompts:

1. **"Am I missing something?" prompt** - Steelman opposition arguments
2. **"Calibrate me" prompt** - Check probability overconfidence
3. **"Post-mortem" prompt** - Distinguish good process from luck

These three prompts alone probably saved me thousands of dollars in the last year. Not because they're complicated. Because they force me to think clearly when my brain wants to take shortcuts.

## Where This Goes From Here

- **Month 1**: Learn language, build system, start tiny
- **Month 2**: Increase sizes gradually, refine rules, track everything
- **Month 3**: Find your edge -- what trades are you best at?
- **Month 6**: Either have a working system or know trading isn't for you

The people who succeed aren't the ones who start with more money or more knowledge. They're the ones who build a system, follow it, and improve it every week. Everything else is noise.
