# If you don't understand AI by the end of this, the next decade will confuse you

**Author:** Anish Moonka (@AnishA_Moonka)
**Date:** March 15, 2026
**Source:** https://x.com/AnishA_Moonka/status/2033128021092073502

## Key Takeaways

- AI should be understood as a five-layer stack (Energy, Chips, Cloud, Models, Applications), and most people only pay attention to the top layer (Applications) while 80% of the capital and profit concentration sits in the bottom four layers.
- The four biggest cloud companies (Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Meta) are spending $650-700 billion on capex in 2026, with ~75% ($450B) going directly into AI infrastructure -- buildings, chips, cables, cooling -- not chatbots.
- "Revenue flows up, capital flows down" -- AI application companies like OpenAI are burning billions ($17B projected cash burn in 2026) that flows to infrastructure providers (Azure, Nvidia, TSMC) who capture the highest margins.
- The AI supply chain is dangerously concentrated: Nvidia holds 92% of AI data centre GPUs, TSMC has 70% of foundry market share, and ASML is the sole EUV lithography supplier -- creating both investment opportunity and geopolitical risk.
- Historical parallel to electricity (1880-1920): the companies that won weren't the lightbulb inventors but the power plant builders, copper miners, and generator manufacturers -- the same "Infrastructure Gravity" pattern is repeating with AI.
- The best near-term investment returns are in infrastructure layers (energy, chips, cloud), while application-layer value will dominate long-term once the buildout matures -- similar to how Amazon/Google/Facebook eventually eclipsed Cisco and fiber companies.
- Key risks include capital misallocation ($650B+ spend may outpace revenue), extreme supply chain concentration (one Taiwan earthquake could set back global AI by years), and the DeepSeek efficiency question challenging the "more spend = better AI" assumption.

## One-Line Summary

AI's real value chain is a five-layer infrastructure stack where profits concentrate at the bottom (energy, chips, cloud) while applications get all the attention, and understanding this structure is the key to navigating the largest technology buildout in history.
